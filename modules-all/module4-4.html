Individual Reflective Piece on Secure Software Development Course

  • Individual Reflective Piece on Secure Software Development Course
    Using Rolfe et al.’s (2001) reflective model, I critically reflect on my experience in the Secure Software Development course, focusing on my role as the document reviewer and submission coordinator in a group project while addressing learning outcomes and professional development throughout the module.

    What?
    Secure software development methodology emphasises in creating secure, efficient software through multiple phases and collaborative teamwork (Casola et al., 2024). As the final document reviewer and submission coordinator for the design document group project, I ensured deliverables met academic and security standards, were cohesive, and were submitted on time. My tasks included reviewing technical documentation for clarity and accuracy, coordinating peer feedback submissions, and ensuring that deadlines for assigned tasks were met in a timely manner. Key aspects included applying secure coding practices, aligning diverse team contributions, and navigating virtual collaboration tools, which tested my organisational and interpersonal skills.

    So What?
    This role developed my critical thinking, time management, and communication skills. Initially, I felt confident in my analytical abilities, but I was anxious about ensuring secure code compliance and meeting tight deadlines. Peer feedback commended my thoroughness, but the professor’s feedback suggested I could be more proactive in multiple areas with the system design. Tutor feedback highlighted the need for more transparent communication in feedback loops, prompting me to refine my approach. Gibbs’ (2013) reflective cycle, as cited by Thejll-Madsen T. in the University of Edinburgh’s Reflection Toolkit (2018), revealed that my hesitation to mediate conflicts stemmed from a fear of technical inaccuracies, which would slow down the decision-making process. (Tyng et al., 2017) notes that emotions shape learning outcomes, explaining how anxiety impacted my performance.

    Now What?
    The course significantly advanced my professional skills. Structured timelines have enhanced my time management, a skill I now apply to professional tasks, including project planning. My IT and digital skills were developed through secure collaboration tools, such as WhatsApp, for improved communication, as well as Git and Google Docs. Meanwhile, my interpersonal skills improved through ensuring equitable team contributions, taking accurate meeting minutes, and distributing tasks effectively. I was able to upscale my professional skills, identify strengths in research and ethical awareness , particularly in ensuring secure coding practices, but highlighted a need for growth in problem-solving. My Personal Development Plan (PDP) would include strategies such as technical assertiveness training and attending secure code review workshops in the future. Artefacts, such as annotated code reviews and email correspondence, demonstrate my contributions to secure deliverables and adherence to deadlines, aligning with the course’s aim of fostering effective and secure team development. Goldens' (2023) emphasis on critical reflection helped me contextualise my behaviour, leading to improved assertiveness and collaboration. These artefacts evidence my mastery of learning outcomes, including critical thinking and ethical awareness.
    Collaboration was central, with my participation in virtual discussions and tasks, ensuring secure task alignment. This experience underscored the importance of clear role bifurcation in virtual teams, a skill that is particularly applicable to software development roles. Ethical awareness was upheld by ensuring proper attribution of contributions and adherence to secure coding standards, aligning with academic integrity principles (University of Essex, 2025).
    In conclusion, the course was transformative, not only enhancing my time management, critical thinking, and interpersonal skills but also helping to upskill my software development skills. Rolfe’s (2001) model facilitated deep reflection, revealing actionable insights for professional growth in secure software development.
