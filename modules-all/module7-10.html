Personal Reflective piece for Research Methods and Professional Practice

  • Word Count: 963 words (excluding references)

    What: Describing the Learning Experience
    Throughout this module, I engaged in a variety of activities that shaped my understanding of computing research, statistical analysis, and professional development. In Collaborative Discussion 1, I posted an initial response on generative AI ethics, responded to peers, and summarised key insights, fostering critical thinking about AI governance. Reflective Activity 1 deepened this by prompting a written reflection on ethical considerations in generative AI, highlighting issues like bias and accountability. In Unit 3, I drafted outlines for my Literature Review and Research Proposal, focusing on enhancing cyberbullying detection through hybrid machine learning models. This was refined in Unit 4 through a peer-reviewed Research Proposal outline, which emphasised multimodal and ethical approaches to social media analysis. Collaborative Discussion 2 further honed my ability to articulate and critique research ideas collaboratively.

    Statistical exercises were central to my learning. The Seminar 4 Inferential Statistics Workshop and associated worksheet introduced me to statistical tools and concepts like hypothesis testing and regression analysis. I also completed Statistical Worksheet Submissions and a Charts Example Worksheet, which required applying statistical methods to real-world data. These tasks challenged my analytical skills but were pivotal in building confidence. Finally, Seminar 6: e-Portfolio Preparation guided me in collating my work, including a SWOT analysis, to reflect on my professional growth. These activities collectively addressed the module’s learning outcomes, particularly appraising ethical issues, academic investigation principles, and research methodology.

    So What: Analysing Emotions, Behaviour, and Impact
    The module was both challenging and rewarding, eliciting a range of emotions that shaped my learning. Initially, the Collaborative Discussion 1 and Reflective Activity 1 on AI ethics felt overwhelming due to the complexity of ethical dilemmas in computing. I felt uncertain about balancing innovation with accountability, but engaging with peers’ perspectives helped me clarify my stance. Referencing Floridi (2019), I realised that ethical frameworks must evolve with AI advancements, which shifted my perspective from apprehension to curiosity. This emotional journey encouraged me to approach ethical discussions with greater openness, enhancing my critical thinking.

    The statistical exercises, particularly in Seminar 4 and the Statistical Worksheet Submissions, were initially daunting. My limited knowledge and prior experience with inferential statistics led to frustration when interpreting p-values and confidence intervals. However, testing and completing the Charts Example Worksheet and visualising data trends boosted my confidence. According to Field (2018), practical application is key to mastering statistical concepts, and this resonated with my experience. The iterative process of trial and error taught me resilience, as I sought additional resources, such as online tutorials, to bridge knowledge gaps. This persistence directly improved my analytical outputs, as evidenced by more accurate worksheet submissions.

    The Literature Review and Research Proposal tasks in Units 3 and 4 were intellectually stimulating but exposed my weakness in structuring academic arguments. Feedback from peers during Collaborative Discussion 2 highlighted areas for improvement, such as clarifying research objectives. This feedback, while initially disheartening, motivated me to refine my proposal on cyberbullying detection. The process underscored the importance of iterative refinement in research, aligning with Saunders et al. (2019), who emphasise the role of feedback in robust research design.

    Reflecting on my SWOT analysis from Seminar 6, I identified strengths in collaboration and critical thinking, but recognised time management as a weakness. The pressure to balance multiple tasks led to rushed submissions early in the module, affecting quality. However, creating an action plan helped me prioritise tasks, resulting in a more structured e-Portfolio. This process made me more self-aware, aligning with Rolfe et al.’s (2001) emphasis on critical self-reflection for professional growth.

    Now What: Applying Learning to Future Contexts
    The module has significantly shaped my academic and professional trajectory. My improved statistical analysis skills, developed through seminars and worksheet submissions, will be invaluable in my career as a computing professional. For instance, I can apply regression analysis to evaluate user data in cybersecurity projects, ensuring evidence-based decisions. This aligns with the module’s learning outcome of evaluating research methodologies, as I now appreciate the importance of robust data analysis in validating research findings.

    The research methods process, particularly through the Literature Review and Research Proposal, has equipped me to approach academic investigations systematically. My proposal on cyberbullying detection highlighted the need for ethical considerations in AI-driven solutions, a principle I will carry into future projects. Drawing on Miteu (2024), I plan to integrate ethical frameworks into my research designs to ensure social responsibility in technology development. This addresses the learning outcome of appraising ethical issues in computing.

    Professionally, the SWOT analysis and action plan have clarified my development goals. I aim to improve time management by adopting tools like Trello for project planning, which will enhance my efficiency in collaborative settings. The collaborative discussions taught me the value of diverse perspectives, which I will apply in team-based projects to foster inclusive decision-making. According to Bansal et al. (2019), effective collaboration is critical in research, and I intend to leverage this in future group endeavours.

    The module also reinforced the importance of academic integrity. Using Harvard referencing correctly, as guided by the University of Essex Online, ensured I acknowledged others’ work appropriately, a practice I will maintain to uphold professional standards. The Turnitin Originality Check process further instilled a habit of verifying citations, which I will continue to ensure ethical scholarship.

    In conclusion, this module has been transformative, enhancing my statistical, research, and reflective skills. The challenges I faced, whether grappling with statistics or refining my research proposal, have fostered resilience and critical thinking. Moving forward, I will apply these skills to real-world computing challenges, particularly in ethical AI and cybersecurity, ensuring my work is both technically sound and socially responsible. The structured reflection process, inspired by Rolfe et al. (2001), has provided a framework to evaluate and improve my practice continuously, ensuring lifelong learning and professional growth.
