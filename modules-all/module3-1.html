Unit 1 Activity - What is Law?


  • In his 2019 article, Conor Gearty argues that the rise of state surveillance and cyber power must not come at the expense of fundamental human rights. He stresses that in an era of digital threats and misinformation, liberal democracies must not sacrifice principles like privacy, due process, and free expression for the sake of national security (Gearty, 2019). His point is clear: the legitimacy of any security framework depends on its alignment with human rights norms, particularly in the face of expanding governmental and corporate digital power (Klonick, 2017; Zuboff, 2019). Zeno-Zencovich’s conclusion presents a bold claim: to effectively prevent and respond to cyber harm, we must move beyond the rigid conceptual distinctions between national legal systems and adopt a more integrated legal framework. He critiques the inadequacy of siloed, territorially bound legal systems in addressing the borderless nature of cyber threats (Zeno-Zencovich, 2017). While his vision of eliminating legal distinctions is ambitious, it is also fraught with practical and political complexities. National legal systems are rooted in diverse traditions and sovereignties, making full unification unlikely (DeNardis, 2014). Nonetheless, his argument sheds light on the need for stronger legal harmonization and international cooperation in the digital domain. A hybrid model—preserving legal pluralism while building interoperable frameworks for cybercrime prevention—may be more achievable (Brenner, 2010). Both authors call for a recalibration of law that maintains human rights protections while adapting to the transnational realities of cyber harm.

    References:
    • Brenner, S. (2010). Cybercrime: Criminal Threats from Cyberspace. Praeger.
    • DeNardis, L. (2014). The Global War for Internet Governance. Yale University Press.
    • Gearty, C. (2019). Human Rights in the Digital Age. London Review of Books.
    • Klonick, K. (2017). The New Governors: The People, Rules, and Processes Governing Online Speech. Harvard Law Review, 131(6), 1598–1670.
    • Zeno-Zencovich, V. (2017). Freedom of Expression: A Critical and Comparative Analysis. RomaTrePress.
    • Zuboff, S. (2019). The Age of Surveillance Capitalism. PublicAffairs.
