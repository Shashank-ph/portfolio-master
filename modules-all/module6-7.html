Personal Reflective Piece for Security and Risk Management Module

  • Word Count: 948 (excluding references)

    Introduction:
    In the Security and Risk Management module, I participated in a range of activities designed to build expertise in identifying, analysing, and mitigating risks in information systems. These included theoretical discussions, practical exercises like vulnerability assessments, and collaborative projects, such as the Pampered Pets digitalisation initiative in Units 6 to 11. Adopting Rolfe et al.’s (2001) reflective framework, What (description), So What (analysis), and Now What (future actions), I will reflect on the risk management process, my individual contributions to team activities, my experiences in a development team, and the module's influence on my professional and personal development. This reflection draws on artefacts, collaborative discussions, peer feedback, and self-evaluation to illustrate my growth, aligning with the module's learning outcomes on critical risk analysis and ethical considerations.

    Risk Management Process (What, So What):
    The module provided a structured introduction to risk management, emphasising identification of threats, vulnerability analysis, and mitigation strategies within the current threat landscape. Early on, in Unit 2, I reviewed a case study, focusing on the Role of AI in Risk Management. By Unit 7, I applied this learning to evaluate risk and threat handling in international supply chain organisations, recognising how digitalisation risks could expose sensitive data. In the Pampered Pets project, I used Monte Carlo simulations to quantify supply chain risks, such as finding the probability of quality degradation from suppliers.

    Analysing this progression (So What):
    I realised my initial challenges stemmed from a reliance on surface-level intuition rather than evidence-based synthesis. The module compelled me to gather information from sources like OWASP Top Ten (2025) for categorising vulnerabilities, such as insecure authentication in applications, and Verizon’s (2025) Data Breach Investigations Report for phishing trends, to name a few. This shift fostered critical thinking, as I learned to articulate business impacts, like data exposure leading to GDPR breaches. In the early stages, it made me feel inadequate, but they motivated deeper engagement, enhancing my ability to apply methodologies like CVSS or SSVC scoring to prioritise risks. This process underscored the ethical responsibilities of security professionals, as per the Joint Task Force Interagency Working Group (2020), in balancing technical solutions with legal and social implications.

    Individual Contributions to Team Activities (What, So What):
    My key contributions centred on risk evaluation in group projects. In the Pampered Pets initiative, I was responsible for assessing digitalisation risks and mitigations, including international supply chain vulnerabilities and business continuity strategies. I produced a section on risk evaluation and mitigation. Artefacts, such as my risk matrix and simulation outputs, demonstrate this, alongside peer evaluation where team members rated my work quality and contributions as above average (4/5). However, self-evaluation revealed I sometimes underestimated delegation, scoring myself 4/5 on initiative.

    Reflecting on these (So What):
    I felt a mix of pride and overwhelm; leading the risk analysis boosted my confidence, but conflicting opinions on prioritisation caused anxiety about meeting word limits. Peer feedback praised my cooperative attitude, aligning with my strengths in clear communication and reliability, as noted in the evaluation template. This experience highlighted how my proactive problem-solving, such as aligning my findings to avoid any overlap, contributed to team success. Yet, it exposed a need for better task distribution, as early underestimation of effort led to rushed revisions. Drawing on prior solo experiences, like analysing OWASP vulnerabilities, I learned to integrate creative input with structured delegation, refining my collaboration skills and reinforcing the value of individual accountability in group outcomes.

    Teamwork Experience (What, So What):
    By working in a four-member group, I experienced the dynamics of virtual collaboration in development projects. We resolved these issues with regular meetings, maintaining alignment despite time zone challenges. The peer evaluation template captured this by providing ratings for attendance and discussions, as well as a self-score for cooperation, which reflects active listening.

    Examining this (So What):
    The group activity experience evoked frustration from clashing views, rooted in my desire for control, but it taught emotional resilience and the benefits of diverse perspectives. Belbin's (2010) team roles helped me identify as a 'Shaper', who pushes for progress but sometimes dominates. Positive behaviours, such as timely completions were valuable, while rare, delayed communications due to scheduling conflicts temporarily hindered momentum, as noted in feedback. This reinforced the collaborative essence of security work, where synthesising inputs from multiple sources leads to robust solutions. Overall, it improved my conflict resolution and adaptability, essential for ethical team practices in high-stakes environments like critical infrastructure protection.

    Professional and Personal Development (What, So What, Now What):
    The module has enhanced my technical proficiency. Personally, it improved time management, as evidenced by meeting Pampered Pets' deadlines after initial setbacks, and boosted confidence in presenting ethical analyses, like AI governance frameworks from Kumar et al. (2025).

    Analysing the impact (So What):
    These experiences transformed my reactive mindset into a proactive one, emphasising legal and social issues in security. Researching AI ethics through UNESCO (2021) reshaped my vision toward roles in secure software development. Emotionally, overcoming struggles built resilience, while peer praise for supportive attitudes affirmed my growth in empathy.

    Looking ahead (Now What):
    I will apply these insights by pursuing the ISO 27001 Lead Auditor certification, which I will add to my PDP. To address areas for improvement, such as active participation, I plan to seek diverse team roles in future projects. Continued reflection using Rolfe's model will ensure I refine delegation and adaptability, preparing me for real-world challenges in information security.

    Conclusion:
    This module has profoundly shaped my abilities, from risk analysis to ethical teamwork. Through Rolfe's framework, I've pinpointed growth areas, supported by artefacts and feedback, and committed to ongoing development. These skills will propel my career, with the e-portfolio evidencing my journey toward becoming a competent security professional.
