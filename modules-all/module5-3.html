    Peer Review 1: individual essay – 2707053319
    This essay presents a thoughtful and structured analysis of the human factors impacting cybersecurity at Bayzat, a UAE-based startup. The writer demonstrates a solid grasp of relevant concepts, such as insider threats, employee awareness, and organisational culture, aligning well with the learning outcome of reviewing human behaviour in cybersecurity (Alshaikh, 2020).
    The case-specific focus on Bayzat and its HR/Insurtech context strengthens the application of theory to a realistic business scenario, meeting expectations for socio-technical risk analysis (McEvoy et al., 2019)).
    The recent insights into user training and leadership’s role in security culture were well presented. The hypothetical example regarding phishing adds clarity and practical relevance. However, the essay would benefit from greater criticality. For instance, while it highlights problems such as a lack of training and access control, it misses the opportunity to critique the absence of structured onboarding or behavioural monitoring systems (Saxena et al., 2020). The presentation is decent, with few spelling or grammatical issues, as per British English language standards. Moreover, citation formatting should consistently follow the University of Essex Online (UoEO) Harvard style. Overall, the essay almost meets academic expectations but demonstrates a good understanding of risk from both technical and behavioural standpoints.


    References:
    1.Alshaikh, M. (2020) ‘Developing cybersecurity culture to influence employee behavior: A practice perspective’, Computers & Security, 98, p. 102003. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cose.2020.102003. [Accessed on 5th July 2025]
    2.McEvoy, T.R. et al., (2019) ‘Deriving Cyber Security Risks from Human and Organizational Factors – A Socio-technical Approach’, Complex Systems Informatics and Modeling Quarterly, (18), pp. 47–64. Available at: https://doi.org/10.7250/csimq.2019-18.03.

    Peer Review 2: Turnitin ID: 2705903654
    The essay presents a nuanced and well-researched examination of human factors that compromise cybersecurity within MediSure, a healthcare startup. The discussion of risk perception, routinised non-compliance, and attentional fragmentation demonstrates strong knowledge of human behaviour in cyber contexts, directly aligning with the learning outcomes (Ifinedo, 2012). The use of high-quality academic sources and current empirical studies supports a critical understanding of the socio-technical risks involved, as suggested by Weickert, Joinson and Craggs (2023).
    One of the essay’s strengths is its framing of human error as systemic rather than individual, showing how performance pressures might embed insecurity into routine operations (Chowdhury et al., 2019). However, a key weakness is the limited attention given to organisational responsibility for long-term cultural development. While the essay critiques short-term pressures, it does not address how leadership practices, onboarding processes, or continuous learning structures could shape a more resilient security culture (Pollini et al., 2021).
    The word count slightly exceeds the limit, nevertheless, the presentation and referencing follow UoEO Harvard style well, and the language is professional and transparent. Overall, this is a critically strong and well-structured piece that accurately reflects the complexity of cybersecurity risks emerging from human behaviour in high-pressure digital healthcare environments.
