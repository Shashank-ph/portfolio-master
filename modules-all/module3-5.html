    Government Abuse of Cyberspace Laws and Human Rights Violations

    Governments can misuse cyberspace laws to suppress dissent, restrict freedom of expression, and conduct mass surveillance under the pretext of national security. While regulations like the GDPR aim to protect privacy, some states exploit vague cybercrime laws to justify censorship and human rights violations.
    For example, China's Cybersecurity Law mandates data localisation and grants the state access to private data, thereby limiting free speech (Qi, Shao and Zheng, 2018). Similarly, Russia’s “Sovereign Internet” law empowers the government to sever global internet access to suppress opposition (Litvinenko, 2021). Authoritarian regimes often use cybercrime legislation to target journalists and activists. India faces multiple challenges in tracing encrypted messages due to the vast amount of data usage on the internet, which undermines privacy rights (Raghav and Marwaha, 2023)
    Mass surveillance is a significant concern, as highlighted by the U.S. PRISM program, which revealed extensive data collection by intelligence agencies and raised ethical questions about privacy (Anderson, 2020).While cybersecurity laws are essential, their misuse can erode fundamental freedoms. International oversight and stronger legal frameworks, such as those in the GDPR, are vital in preventing abuse while striking a balance between digital security and human rights.
