Cyber Crime Analysis – Cyber Hacking in India & UK

  • Description and Explanation of the Crime
    Hacking is the unauthorised access, modification, or exploitation of computer systems, networks, or data (Legal Guidance, 2024). Worldwide, hacking can range from minor unauthorised access to critical government and financial institution attacks. Common motivations include financial gain, political activism (hacktivism), espionage, or simply the challenge of bypassing security (Chng et al., 2022).
    In India, hacking incidents have risen due to rapid digitalisation and weak cybersecurity infrastructure (Mishra, 2023). High-profile breaches, such as the 2018 Aadhaar database leak, highlight vulnerabilities in government and corporate networks. In the UK, cyberattacks have targeted banks, healthcare systems (e.g., the 2017 WannaCry ransomware attack on the NHS), and even national security networks.

    Unique Characteristics of Cyber Hacking
    Hacking differs from traditional crimes due to:
    1. Anonymity & Attribution – Attackers can conceal identities using proxies, VPNs, and encrypted communication.
    2. Global Reach – Hackers can attack from anywhere, making jurisdictional enforcement difficult.
    3. Automation & Scalability – A single attack can impact thousands, as seen in botnet-based DDoS attacks.
    4. Evolving Tactics – Unlike physical crimes, hacking methods evolve rapidly, often outpacing legal frameworks.

    Comparison with Offline Crimes
    Unlike traditional crimes, hacking lacks a direct offline equivalent. However, analogies can be drawn with offences such as trespassing (unauthorised entry into private spaces) and vandalism (destruction of data or networks). Cyber hacking, however, amplifies the impact by enabling massive-scale attacks, cross-border operations, and long-term economic and national security consequences.

    Legal Frameworks: India & UK
    • India: The Information Technology (IT) Act, 2000, criminalises hacking under Section 66, penalising unauthorised access and data theft. However, enforcement is weak due to outdated laws and limited cyber forensics capabilities (Singh and Singh, 2023).
    • UK: The Computer Misuse Act of 1990 is the primary legislation. It categorises offences such as unauthorised access (Section 1), intent to commit further crimes (Section 2), and impairing systems (Section 3). The UK also aligns with the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), imposing strict cybersecurity compliance (Watts, 2025).

    Issues Concerning Burden & Standard of Proof

    • Burden of Proof: The prosecution must prove unauthorised access, intent, and resulting damage. Since hackers operate remotely and erase evidence, proving intent can be challenging (Aravindakshan, 2021).
    • Standards of Proof: In criminal cases, the standard is "beyond reasonable doubt," which is difficult due to encrypted transactions and anonymised networks (Albanese, 2023). • Admissibility of Evidence: Courts in both countries recognise digital forensics, but chain-of-custody issues, data integrity, and jurisdictional conflicts often complicate admissibility (Aravindakshan, 2021).

    Conclusion
    Hacking is a rapidly evolving cybercrime with significant national and economic implications. While India and the UK have legal frameworks, enforcement remains challenging due to anonymity, jurisdictional limitations, and evolving attack methods. Strengthening cross-border collaboration, digital forensics, and updating legal frameworks are crucial to combat cyber hacking effectively.
