Case Study Review: LinkedIn GDPR Compliance Inquiry (2018) – Behavioural Analysis and Targeted Advertising

  • Selected Case Study
    The 2018 LinkedIn inquiry (IN-18-08-3), initiated by the Data Protection Commission (DPC) following a complaint from La Quadrature Du Net, examined LinkedIn Ireland Unlimited Company’s processing of personal data for behavioural analysis and targeted advertising (BA & TA), highlighting non-compliance with GDPR’s lawfulness, fairness, and transparency principles (Data Protection Commission, 2024).

    What is the specific aspect of GDPR that your case study addresses?
    This case study primarily addresses Article 5(1)(a) of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which requires personal data to be processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently in relation to the data subject (European Union, 2016). It also engages Article 6(1), concerning lawful bases for processing (e.g., consent, legitimate interests, and contractual necessity), and Articles 13(1)(c) and 14(1)(c), which mandate clear disclosure of processing purposes and legal bases for data collected directly or from third parties. The inquiry focused on LinkedIn’s use of first-party data (e.g., user profiles) and third-party data (e.g., via LinkedIn Insight Tag) for BA & TA, finding deficiencies in consent validity, legitimate interest balancing, and transparency of data processing practices (Data Protection Commission, 2024).

    How was it resolved?
    The DPC initiated the inquiry on 20 August 2018, following a complaint about LinkedIn’s processing of personal data for BA & TA without valid legal bases (Data Protection Commission, 2024). The resolution involved the DPC issuing a reprimand, ordering LinkedIn to bring its processing and Privacy Policy into compliance with GDPR Articles 5(1)(a), 6(1), 13(1)(c), and 14(1)(c), and imposing fines totalling €310 million for infringements related to invalid reliance on consent (€105 million), legitimate interests and contractual necessity (€110 million), and transparency failures (€95 million) (Data Protection Commission, 2024). LinkedIn had already ceased relying on contractual necessity (Article 6(1)(b)) by January 2020, shifting to legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f)), but the DPC found these bases invalid due to insufficient user information and imbalanced rights considerations (Data Protection Commission, 2024; European Union, 2016). The case underscored the need for precise, granular consent mechanisms and transparent privacy policies, influencing subsequent GDPR enforcement.

    If this was your organisation, what steps would you take as an Information Security Manager to mitigate the issue?
    As an Information Security Manager, I would implement a multi-layered strategy aligned with ISO 27001 and GDPR to ensure compliant data processing for BA & TA:

    1. Conduct a Comprehensive Risk Assessment: Perform a data flow mapping exercise to identify all data sources (first- and third-party) used for BA & TA, using tools like Nessus or OWASP ZAP to assess vulnerabilities in data processing pipelines. Engage legal, IT, and privacy teams to evaluate compliance with Article 6(1), focusing on high-risk data categories (e.g., inferred gender or age) (European Union, 2016).
    2. Implement Technical Controls: Redesign consent mechanisms to ensure they are freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous (Article 6(1)(a)), using clear, equally prominent opt-in/opt-out buttons and detailed disclosures about third-party data sources (e.g., Insight Tag). Apply pseudonymisation to inferred data and restrict micro-targeting to comply with Article 5(1)(c) data minimisation (ENISA, 2019).
    3. Enhance Organisational Measures: Roll out mandatory GDPR training, focusing on Article 25 (data protection by design) and fair processing principles, including simulations of non-compliant BA & TA scenarios. Establish a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) process for all advertising features to meet Article 35 requirements (Hoepman, 2020).
    4. Incident Response and Monitoring: Deploy Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) tools (e.g., Splunk) to monitor data processing activities and detect non-compliant practices. Conduct quarterly audits and penetration tests to ensure ongoing Article 32 compliance (Tankard, 2016).
    5. Stakeholder Engagement and Reporting: Update the Privacy Policy to clearly link data categories, processing purposes, and legal bases, ensuring compliance with Articles 13(1)(c) and 14(1)(c). Notify the DPC within 72 hours of any detected issues (Article 33) and communicate transparently with users, embedding compliance metrics in KPIs (MetricStream, 2025).
    These steps could reduce non-compliance risks by 40–60%, based on industry benchmarks, fostering fair and transparent data processing (ENISA, 2019). This analysis contributes to my e-portfolio by demonstrating GDPR principal application and proactive risk management skills, preparing me for team discussions on comparative GDPR enforcement in next week’s seminar.
